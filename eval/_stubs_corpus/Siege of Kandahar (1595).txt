The Siege of Kandahar was a siege by the Uzbeks between 1597 and 1598, which later led to the Mughal Empire capturing Kandahar without a fight.


== Background ==
By the early 1590s, the Bukhara Khanate under Abdullah Khan II had reached the zenith of its power, having successfully annexed major parts of Khorasan, including Herat (1588) and Mashhad (1589). However, the southern frontier, specifically the strategic city of Kandahar, remained a contentious zone.
Kandahar was at that time a peripheral province of the Safavid Empire, governed by the Safavid prince Mozaffar-Hosayn Mirza. The city was geographically isolated from the Safavid capital in western Iran, making it vulnerable to the expanding Uzbek and Mughal interests. For the Shaybanids, Kandahar was the final key to securing Khorasan; for the Mughals, it was the "Gateway to India" and a vital bulwark for the defense of Kabul.


== The Shaybanid Campaign and Siege (1594) ==
In 1594, Abdullah Khan II authorized a major campaign to eliminate the last vestiges of Safavid influence in the south. The expedition was led by his ambitious nephew, Din Muhammad Sultan, who had already proven himself in the battles for Sistan.
Din Muhammad Sultan’s forces successfully overran the surrounding districts of Kandahar, defeating local Safavid commanders like Mustafa Khan Kangarlu. The Uzbek cavalry eventually laid a tight siege to the fortress of Kandahar. The siege was highly effective, cutting off all supply routes and putting the governor, Muzaffar Husayn Mirza, in a desperate position. The Safavid central government under Shah Abbas I was preoccupied with internal rebellions and could not provide any reinforcements.


== The Diplomatic Withdrawal ==
Despite the military advantage held by the Uzbeks, the fate of the siege was decided not by the sword, but by high diplomacy. Abdullah Khan II, a seasoned statesman, grew wary of the potential consequences of occupying Kandahar. 
He was well aware that Akbar The Great of the Mughal Empire viewed Kandahar as an ancestral territory and a strategic necessity. Abdullah Khan II sought to maintain a stable relationship with Akbar to prevent a two-front war (against the Safavids and Mughals simultaneously). In a series of diplomatic exchanges, Abdullah Khan II expressed his view that the Hindu Kush and Kandahar should serve as a natural buffer and boundary between the two Sunni empires.
Due to a border agreement with the Mughal Empire, Abdullah Khan II immediately ordered Din Muhammad to lift the siege, as Kandahar was to serve as a buffer zone between the Mughal Empire and the Khanate of Bukhara.


== Capture of Kandahar without a fight (1595) ==
The withdrawal of the Shaybanid army left Muzaffar Husayn Mirza in a precarious "power vacuum." Though the immediate Uzbek threat had retreated, he knew that the Safavids could no longer protect him and that the Uzbeks would eventually return.
Realizing that his position was untenable, Muzaffar Husayn Mirza chose to surrender the city to the Mughals rather than face another Uzbek siege or execution by the Safavids for incompetence. He entered into secret negotiations with Akbar's court.
In April 1595, a Mughal expeditionary force led by Shah Beg Khan arrived at Kandahar. True to the agreement, Muzaffar Husayn Mirza peacefully opened the gates and surrendered the keys of the citadel. There was no bloodshed; the transition of power was purely administrative. Muzaffar Husayn Mirza subsequently relocated to the Mughal court in Agra, where Akbar granted him a high-ranking mansab of 5,000 and the lucrative jagir (estate) of Sambhal.


== Strategic Aftermath ==
Although the peaceful capture of Kandahar by the Mughal Empire was unexpected for the Khanate of Bukhara, it did, to some extent, lead to a revision of the peace treaty between the two states.

The Hindu Kush Boundary: Following the annexation, Abdullah Khan II and Akbar formalized their boundary along the Hindu Kush. This "gentleman's agreement" stabilized Central-South Asian relations for several years.
The Safavid Loss: For the Safavids, the loss of Kandahar without a fight was a humiliation that Shah Abbas I would not forget. It set the stage for the future Mughal–Safavid War (1622–1623).
Shaybanid-Mughal Relations: Abdullah Khan II attempted to turn the loss into a diplomatic opportunity by proposing a joint Shaybanid-Mughal partition of Iran. However, Akbar, having already secured Kandahar, saw little reason to assist the Uzbeks in becoming even more powerful.
